Here are some options, roughly organized by tone:

**Direct and descriptive:**
- *Credo Ergo Sum: A Reflexive Fixed-Point Theory of the Hard Problem of Consciousness*
- *The Hard Problem as Identity: Reflexive Closure, Self-Validating Belief, and the Constitution of Subjective Experience*
- *Dissolving the Hard Problem: A Fixed-Point Identity Theory of Consciousness*

**More concise:**
- *The Hard Problem at the Fixed Point*
- *Credo Ergo Sum: Belief, Self-Reference, and the Hard Problem of Consciousness*
- *The Belief That Makes Itself True: A Reflexive Theory of the Hard Problem*

**Leading with the core move:**
- *The Hard Problem as a Fixed-Point Identity*
- *Consciousness Without the Gap: Reflexive Closure and the Hard Problem*
- *What the Hard Problem Is Missing: An Identity Theory via Self-Referential Belief*

**Bolder:**
- *The Hard Problem Dissolves at the Fixed Point*
- *Cornering the Hard Problem: Consciousness as Reflexive Closure*

My instinct is that you want "Credo Ergo Sum" in there somewhere — it's memorable, it signals the relationship to Descartes immediately, and it tells the reader this is a philosophical thesis with ambition. Something like:

*Credo Ergo Sum: Cornering the Hard Problem at the Fixed Point*

Short, signals the core move, and "cornering" is honest — it's what you actually do, rather than claiming to solve it.
