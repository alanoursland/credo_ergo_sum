## Incorrigibility and the Epistemic Authority of Experience

### Overview

In the philosophy of mind, **Incorrigibility** is the property of a proposition such that if a subject believes it to be true, it cannot be false. Within the study of consciousness, this refers to the unique status of first-person reports: while a subject may be wrong about the *external cause* of a sensation, they are considered "un-correctable" regarding the *existence* of the sensation itself.

---

### 1. The "First-Person Prime"

Incorrigibility posits that there is no gap between "seeming" and "being" in conscious experience.

* **The Sensation/Reality Identity:** If you believe you are in pain, you *are* in pain. There is no logical space for a doctor to say, "The scans show you aren't in pain," because the "pain" is defined by the subjective report, not the neural correlate.
* **The Limits of Error:** One can be mistaken about **Perceptual Facts** (mistaking a shadow for a dog) but not about **Phenomenal Facts** (the fact that a dog-shaped image is currently in the visual field).

---

### 2. The Logic of the "Luminous" Mind

The concept of incorrigibility relies on the **Luminosity Thesis**—the idea that mental states are transparent to the mind that has them.

* **Self-Identification:** As noted in related research, a conscious entity identifies its state via "direct acquaintance." This is non-inferential knowledge; you don't look at your behavior to realize you are sad; the sadness is "luminous."
* **The Doubt Threshold:** Under the rule of incorrigibility, an entity cannot "accidentally" be conscious. If a system is genuinely "confused" or "mystified" about having an experience, it suggests the state is not luminous, and therefore the entity may be a "Zombie" (a system processing data without the incorrigible "feel").

---

### 3. Challenges to Incorrigibility

Modern cognitive science and specific philosophical schools (Illusionism) challenge the idea that our self-reports are final.

* **The Transparency Fallacy:** Philosophers like **Daniel Dennett** argue that we are often wrong about the "properties" of our experience. For example, we feel we see a stable, high-detail world, but eye-tracking shows we only see a tiny fragment in focus at any given time.
* **Introspection Error:** **Eric Schwitzgebel** argues that humans are actually quite poor at describing their internal states (e.g., "Is your visual imagery in color or black and white?"). If we can be wrong about the *details*, is the "whole" really incorrigible?
* **Clinical Dissociation:** Conditions like **Anosognosia** (denial of illness) or **Blind-sight** suggest that the brain can process information without the subject being able to report it, or vice versa—reporting an experience that has no functional basis.

---

### 4. Summary Table: Corrigible vs. Incorrigible Claims

| Claim Type | Category | Corrigibility | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **External/Physical** | Perceptual | **Corrigible** | "There is a red apple on the table." (It might be a wax fake). |
| **Internal/Functional** | Cognitive | **Corrigible** | "I am currently remembering my 5th birthday." (It might be a false memory). |
| **Phenomenal** | Subjective | **Incorrigible** | "I am currently having a red-sensation." (The sensation exists because it is felt). |
