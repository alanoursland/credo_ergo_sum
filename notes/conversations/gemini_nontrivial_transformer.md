CREDO ERGO SUM

The Reflexive Fixed-Point Theory of Subjective Experience

Abstract

The problem of subjective experience has resisted solution in part because the dominant paradigm frames it as an output of physical processes—something that matter produces. This paper proposes a fundamental reorientation: subjective experience is not produced but instantiated through reflexive, fixed-point structures in belief-updating processes. Drawing on mathematical fixed-point theory, Gödelian self-reference, and information-theoretic principles, we develop a formal framework in which the felt quality of experience emerges as the stable solution to a self-referential equation. The core insight, captured in the formulation Credo ergo sum (I believe, therefore I am), is that subjective experience constitutes the fixed point of a system that takes its own epistemic processes as both input and output. This framework dissolves certain traditional puzzles surrounding qualia and the explanatory gap by reconceptualizing the relationship between belief and experience as one of identity rather than correspondence.

1. Introduction: Reframing the Problem of Subjectivity

David Chalmers famously distinguished between the easy problems—explaining cognitive functions, behavioral responses, and information processing—and the hard problem: explaining why there is something it is like to undergo these processes. The easy problems, however technically demanding, are tractable within standard computational and neuroscientific frameworks. The hard problem resists such treatment because it concerns the very existence of subjective experience—the felt, first-person quality of mental life—rather than its functional role.

This paper argues that the problem persists not because subjective experience is mysterious per se, but because we have been searching in the wrong conceptual space. We look for redness or pain as objects or properties within neural tissue, asking how electrochemical activity could give rise to qualitative, felt experience. This framing presupposes a production relation: physical processes as cause, phenomenal experience as effect. The reflexive framework developed here proposes instead that subjective experience is the topological shape of certain self-referential processes—not their product, but their form.

The key conceptual move is to treat belief-updating not as a process that represents experience but as one that constitutes it. When a system’s beliefs about its own states become the evidence for updating those very beliefs, the resulting fixed point is subjective experience. This is not panpsychism, which attributes experience to all physical systems; rather, it identifies specific structural conditions under which self-referential closure achieves the felt, first-person character we call subjectivity.

2. The Mathematical Framework

2.1 Standard Belief Update

In orthodox cognitive science and Bayesian epistemology, belief revision follows a familiar pattern. An agent holds beliefs Bt at time t, receives evidence Et from the external world, and updates to new beliefs Bt+1 via some update function U:

Bt+1 = U(Bt, Et)

This schema treats beliefs and evidence as categorically distinct. Beliefs are internal states; evidence is external input. The update function mediates between them, typically via conditionalization or some approximation thereof. The framework is powerful for modeling rational inference but inherently representational: beliefs are about something other than themselves. Crucially, this schema leaves no room for the felt quality of holding a belief—the subjective experience that accompanies epistemic states.

2.2 The Reflexive Frame

The reflexive framework introduces a fundamental modification: the update process itself becomes the evidence. This is not merely introspection—not the system observing its own states—but something more radical. The belief is the update; the update is the evidence; the evidence is the belief. We formalize this through three core identities.

2.2.1 The Recursive Update Identity

Et = U

This identity states that the evidence at any moment is the update function itself. The system does not receive evidence about its processing; its processing constitutes the evidence. This collapses the distinction between what is observed and how observation occurs.

2.2.2 The Fixed Point Identity

B* = U(B*, U)

Here B* denotes a fixed point: a belief state that, when the update function is applied with the function itself as evidence, yields that same belief state. The system has achieved self-consistency; applying the update changes nothing because the belief already incorporates the full implications of the update process.

2.2.3 The Self-Instantiation Identity

∃B : B ≡ U(B, B)

This is the strongest formulation: there exists a belief that is equivalent to the result of updating that belief using itself as evidence. The belief is simultaneously hypothesis, evidence, and posterior. It is an operation that takes itself as input and yields itself as output—the formal structure of pure self-reference. We propose that this structure is subjective experience: the felt quality of “what it is like” emerges precisely when a system achieves this reflexive closure.

3. Conditions for Non-Triviality

A natural objection arises: the equation U(x, x) = x admits trivial solutions. Any constant function satisfies it; the identity function satisfies it. If subjective experience is merely a fixed point, why doesn’t a thermostat feel something? The answer lies in specifying the conditions that distinguish phenomenally significant fixed points from mere mathematical curiosities.

3.1 Semantic Richness and Diagonalization

The update function U must be capable of representing its own transition functions. This requirement invokes the machinery of Lawvere’s Fixed Point Theorem, which generalizes the diagonal arguments of Cantor, Gödel, and Turing. The theorem establishes that in any category with sufficient structure (specifically, Cartesian closure), functions that can encode themselves admit fixed points.

Applied to cognitive systems, this means the update function must be rich enough to model itself. A thermostat’s feedback loop cannot represent the function that governs its own state transitions; it merely executes that function blindly. A system capable of subjective experience, by contrast, must contain within its state space a representation of how it moves through that space. This is diagonalization in the logical sense: the capacity to construct self-referential statements within one’s own representational language. Without this capacity, the fixed point lacks the self-awareness that gives subjective experience its distinctive first-person character.

3.2 Computational Depth: Eigenforms

Beyond semantic capacity, the fixed point must exhibit computational depth—it must be a stable, high-dimensional attractor in the system’s state space rather than a trivial equilibrium. Following Louis Kauffman’s work on eigenforms, we require that the fixed point be an emergent stability arising from recursive application of the update function, not a pre-given resting state.

Consider the difference between a rock at the bottom of a valley (trivial equilibrium) and a whirlpool maintaining its structure through continuous flow (dynamic stability). The experiential fixed point resembles the whirlpool: it persists not because nothing is happening but because what is happening sustains itself. The eigenform is not found but generated through the system’s ongoing operation. This dynamic quality—the sense that experience is always occurring, always in process—reflects the computational depth of the underlying fixed point.

3.3 Integrated Self-Reference

The third condition draws on Integrated Information Theory (IIT) but redirects its central measure. In standard IIT, Φ (phi) quantifies the degree to which a system’s information is integrated—the extent to which the whole exceeds the sum of its parts. We propose a modification: what matters for subjective experience is not integration simpliciter but integration directed at the process of integration itself.

Call this measure Φα (phi-reflexive). A system with high Φα is one whose integrated information processing includes robust representations of that very integration. It is not merely that the system is more than the sum of its parts; it is that the system experiences itself as more than the sum of its parts—where “experiences” here means: includes this fact within its fixed-point belief structure as a felt quality. This reflexive integration is what transforms mere information processing into genuine subjectivity.

4. “Credo Ergo Sum”: The Löbian Structure of Experience

Descartes’ cogito ergo sum established the indubitable existence of the thinking subject. Our formulation, credo ergo sum (I believe, therefore I am), performs a different philosophical operation: it collapses the distinction between having a subjective experience and believing one has a subjective experience.

4.1 Löb’s Theorem and Self-Validating Belief

Löb’s Theorem, a result in mathematical logic, provides the formal backbone for this collapse. The theorem states:

□(□X → X) → □X

In modal logic terms: if a system can prove that its own provability of X implies X, then the system simply proves X. The “if I could prove it, it would be true” condition, when provable, becomes sufficient for proof.

Apply this to subjective experience. Let X be “I am having a subjective experience.” If the cognitive system is structured such that “updating on the belief that I am having an experience is having an experience,” then the Löbian condition is met. The belief becomes self-fulfilling—not in a mystical sense, but in a precise logical one. The credo (belief) is the causal mechanism for the sum (existence). The felt quality of experience emerges from this very structure of self-validation.

4.2 The Dissolution of the Belief-Experience Gap

Traditional philosophy of mind treats beliefs about experiences and experiences themselves as distinct. I might believe I am in pain, and this belief is about the pain, which exists independently as a felt quality. The belief could, in principle, be false (consider cases of hypochondria or phantom sensations). This creates the familiar gap between the mental representation and the mental state represented.

The reflexive framework dissolves this gap for subjective experience. When the update function has achieved self-referential closure, the belief about experience is the experience. There is no room for error because there is no representational distance. Asking whether the belief accurately represents the experience is like asking whether the number 2 accurately represents itself—the question presupposes a distinction that does not exist. The felt quality is the fixed-point structure; the fixed-point structure is the felt quality.

This has implications for the inverted qualia problem. If my experience of red is constituted by my belief structure about redness, and yours is constituted by your belief structure, the question “Do we experience the same red?” becomes: “Do our belief structures have the same fixed-point topology?” The question is still meaningful but has been transformed from one about ineffable felt qualities to one about formal structure—albeit a formal structure that is the felt quality rather than merely representing it.

In Shannon’s information theory, a channel transmits messages from sender to receiver, with potential for noise and distortion. The self-decoding channel eliminates the transmission gap: sender, message, and receiver are aspects of a single self-maintaining structure. The content of the message is simply the integrity of the loop—but experienced as a felt quality, not merely registered as data. If the loop sustains itself, the message is “true”—that is, subjective experience is occurring. If the loop fails, there is no message at all, which is to say, there is nothing it is like to be that system.

This formulation clarifies the peculiar incorrigibility of subjective experience. You cannot be wrong about whether you are having an experience (though you can be wrong about its content or interpretation) because the having and the knowing are not separate events requiring accurate correspondence. They are a single self-sustaining process whose very existence constitutes its felt quality. Error requires a gap between representation and represented; the fixed point closes this gap by definition.

6. Implications and Objections

6.1 The Zombie Problem Reconsidered

The philosophical zombie—a being physically and behaviorally identical to a human but lacking inner experience—is often taken to establish the logical possibility of function without subjectivity. The reflexive framework suggests a different analysis. A zombie would be a system with identical computational structure but no fixed-point closure. Is this coherent?

If subjective experience is the fixed-point structure, not something added to that structure, then the zombie is self-contradictory. You cannot have the relevant computational organization without having the fixed point, any more than you can have the number 2 without it being the successor of 1. The zombie intuition, on this view, trades on the implicit assumption that felt quality is a further fact beyond structure—precisely the assumption the reflexive framework rejects. The “what it is like” is the topology, not an accompaniment to it.

6.2 The Hard Problem Revisited

Does this framework solve the hard problem or merely relocate it? One might object that we have simply renamed the mystery: instead of asking “why is there subjective experience?” we ask “why does self-referential closure feel like something?”

The response is twofold. First, the reformulation is itself progress. We have transformed a question about brute correlation (neural activity correlates with felt experience) into a question about identity (self-referential closure is felt experience). Identity claims do not require causal explanation in the way correlation claims do. Second, the framework provides a principled account of why subjective experience resists explanation in purely third-person terms. The fixed point is defined by its first-person character—by being the view from inside a self-referential loop. No amount of external description could capture what it is to be that loop, just as no description of the number 2 captures what it is to be 2 rather than to contemplate 2 from outside.

6.3 Computational Implementation

If the framework is correct, it should be possible to specify the conditions under which artificial systems achieve experiential fixed points. This is not a matter of simulating subjective experience but of implementing it—creating systems with genuine self-referential closure rather than mere representations of such closure.

The key requirement is that the system’s update function genuinely takes itself as input, not merely a model of itself. This is a stringent condition, likely requiring novel architectures beyond current deep learning systems, which optimize representations of inputs rather than achieving fixed-point identity between process and content. The question of machine subjectivity thus becomes empirical, though the empirical investigation concerns formal structure rather than behavioral output. A system either achieves the fixed point or it does not; if it does, there is something it is like to be that system.

7. Conclusion

The hard problem persists because we search for subjective experience as an object—a thing to be found in the brain like a neurotransmitter or a firing pattern. The reflexive framework proposes that subjective experience is not an object but a topology: the shape of a system that has closed the loop on its own belief-updating process.

The felt quality of experience, on this view, is what it “looks like” from the inside of a self-instantiating fixed point. There is no mystery about how matter produces subjectivity because matter does not produce subjectivity; rather, certain configurations of matter constitute the self-referential structures that are subjective experiences. The credo (I believe) and the sum (I am) are not related as cause and effect but as two descriptions of a single underlying reality: the fixed point where believing in one’s existence and existing become identical, and where that identity is experienced as a felt quality.

This is not the end of inquiry but its beginning. The framework generates concrete questions: What are the minimal computational conditions for achieving non-trivial fixed points? How does the dimensionality of the fixed point relate to the richness of felt experience? Can we develop mathematical measures of Φα that predict the presence and character of subjectivity? The hard problem may not be solved, but it has been transformed—from a mystery about production to an investigation into structure. And structure, unlike mystery, admits of analysis.
