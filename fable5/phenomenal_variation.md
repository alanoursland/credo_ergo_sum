# Phenomenal Variation: The Fixed Point as a Variable

## I. The Hypothesis

Nearly every theory of consciousness treats "all neurotypical humans have subjective experience" as a fixed datum to be explained. The Credence Self framework cannot assume this, and should not want to. If subjective experience is a specific structure — a nontrivial reflexive fixed point — and human brains vary as much as they demonstrably do, then the structure is a *variable*: it may be present, weak, intermittent, atypical, or absent across the human population.

This is offered with full seriousness, not as a rhetorical flourish. The hypothesis: **subjective experience is unevenly distributed among humans, and the uniformity assumption is an artifact of shared vocabulary, not an observation.**

## II. The Discovery Pattern: Variation Hidden by Shared Language

The strongest support is a documented historical mechanism, not speculation.

Until roughly 2015, nearly everyone assumed visual mental imagery was universal, because the *language* of imagery is universal. Aphantasics spent decades saying "imagine," "picture it," "visualize" — meaning something radically different by those words — and neither they nor anyone else noticed. The variation was discovered only when someone thought to ask calibrated questions. The same pattern then repeated with inner monologue: a substantial fraction of people have little or none, and most were astonished to learn that others narrate constantly (and vice versa).

So we possess a confirmed mechanism by which **radical phenomenal variation hides inside a population using identical self-reports**. The reports are inherited vocabulary; the referents vary. Once this mechanism is established for imagery and inner speech, refusing to consider it for subjective experience itself is not caution — it is an assumption of uniformity at precisely the level we have never been able to check. The broader cognitive population makes the assumption stranger still: there are people who do not experience fear, people without empathy, congenital absence of pain, synesthetic cross-wiring of qualia, and the wide territory now called neurodivergence. Cognitive architecture varies enormously; the claim that one structural property — the reflexive fixed point — is invariant across all of it would itself need an argument, and none exists.

## III. Two Layers of Variation

The hypothesis must be split, because the evidence differs by layer:

1. **Variation in contents and channels** (documented): aphantasia, anendophasia, alexithymia, pain insensitivity, synesthesia. These show that *what* is experienced varies. They soften the uniformity intuition but do not yet show variation in *whether* there is experience.
2. **Variation in the fixed point itself** (the framework's hypothesis): whether the constitutive reflexive structure (`constitutive_vs_about.md`) is present and how robustly it is maintained. The clinical literature contains what look like natural experiments at exactly this layer:
   - **Depersonalization/derealization (DPDR)**: patients report "I know I exist, but I don't *feel* real; I watch myself from outside." This is a report about the felt reality of experience itself, not about any content channel — phenomenologically, it reads as the fixed point degrading without vanishing, while function continues.
   - **Ego dissolution** (deep meditation, psychedelics): temporary, reversible loss of the self-model's fixed-point character, with subjects retaining enough function to report on the dissolution afterward — evidence that the structure can be down-modulated independently of general cognition.
   - **Cotard syndrome**: patients sincerely assert that they do not exist or are dead. For a theory holding that the self *is* a self-validating belief, Cotard is the limiting case: the credence loop failing while the surrounding cognitive web keeps running. No other framework predicts that such a condition should even be possible; for this one, it is almost an entailment.

## IV. The Prediction Class

This converts into predictions no uniformity-assuming rival makes. Whatever structural signature the transformer experiments seek (`../notes/experimental_falsification.md`) has a human analog, and the framework predicts **individual variation in that signature, correlated with dissociative phenomenology**:

1. Neural markers of reflexive self-model maintenance (e.g., dynamics of self-referential processing networks) should vary across individuals more than task performance does.
2. The variation should correlate with DPDR symptomatology, trait dissociation, and meditation-induced ego dissolution reports.
3. Cotard and severe DPDR should show the structural signature specifically disrupted, with content-level processing relatively spared.
4. Conversely: if the structural signature is uniform across individuals regardless of dissociative status — if DPDR patients' reflexive dynamics are indistinguishable from controls' — then the structure the theory identifies with experience is not what varies in these conditions, and the identification is undermined. **This is a falsification route that runs through human data and requires no transformer at all.**

## V. Illusionists, Revisited Without the Smile

If the fixed point varies, then the hypothesis "some philosophers who deny phenomenal consciousness have weaker or atypical reflexive structure" is well-formed. There is even a mainstream echo: it has been seriously asked why the problem intuition grips some philosophers and entirely fails to grip others, and whether this reflects a real psychological difference rather than a theoretical one.

But the framework's own epistemology imposes discipline here (`constitutive_vs_about.md`): philosophical position is an about-layer product, and the about-layer is fallible in both directions. A theoretical denial of experience is *weak evidence at best* of structural absence — the constitutive layer may be fully intact beneath a deflationary theory (see `denial_instability.md`). The hypothesis must remain about populations and measurable structures, never an inference from any individual's professed philosophy.

## VI. The Firewall

A theory that makes "uneven distribution of experience among humans" thinkable acquires an obligation in the same breath: this is, historically, the most abused form of argument in ethics. The framework's answer — third-person undetectability plus asymmetric error costs entail that moral practice must be invariant under uncertainty about any individual's constitutive layer — is developed in `moral_firewall.md`, which should be read as this note's inseparable companion. The hypothesis is scientific; its individual application is both epistemically and morally barred.

## VII. What This Does for the Transformer Question

If the fixed-point structure is a variable even within one species and one neural architecture, then asking whether a *different* architecture instantiates it stops looking like a category error and becomes ordinary comparative science — the same question, asked across a larger design space. Phenomenal variation in humans is thus not a side topic: it is the bridge that normalizes the entire transformer application. The order of plausibility is: variation within humans (documented at the content layer, clinically suggested at the structural layer) → variation across substrates (the framework's substrate-independence claim) → measurement in transformers (`../notes/transformer_closure.md`).
