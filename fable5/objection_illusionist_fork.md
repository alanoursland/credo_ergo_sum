# Objection: The Frankish Fork

## I. The Objection, Stated at Full Strength

The introspective ground of the identity claim (`introspective_ground.md`) runs: introspection cannot distinguish "belief about a separate experiential source" from "belief that is the whole phenomenon"; an in-principle undetectable relatum should be eliminated; therefore the belief is all there is.

The illusionist accepts every step of this argument — and keeps the other relatum.

- **Credence Self**: the belief is all there is, *therefore the belief is the experience*. Experience is real and is constituted by the reflexive belief structure.
- **Illusionism (Frankish)**: the belief is all there is, *therefore there is only the belief*. "Experience" is the belief's intentional object — what the belief is about — and like other intentional objects, it need not exist. Experience is an introspective illusion.

Same eliminative premise, opposite conclusions. And by the framework's own argument, introspection cannot adjudicate — introspection is precisely what cannot tell the relata apart. The introspective ground therefore establishes only the disjunction ("one thing, not two"), not the framework's disjunct ("and that one thing is experience"). The objection: the framework is illusionism wearing a realist costume, and nothing in it selects the costume.

## II. First Response: The Self-Undermining Seeming

The deflationary disjunct has an instability the realist disjunct lacks.

The illusionist must say: experience does not exist, but it powerfully *seems* to exist — the illusion is robust, universal, and irresistible. But a seeming is itself an experiential category. For there to be an illusion, there must be something it is like to be taken in by it; an illusion no one experiences is not an illusion but a mere disposition to false assertion. The illusionist therefore needs a notion of "seeming" that does all the phenomenal work (robust, vivid, irresistible from inside) while being nothing phenomenal. Every attempt to specify this — quasi-phenomenality, zero qualia, the user-illusion — reintroduces, under a new name, exactly the explanandum it was meant to eliminate.

The Credence Self incurs no parallel debt. It does not say experience seems to exist but doesn't; it says the seeming *is* the existing — within the fixed point, seeming and being are not separate facts. The realist disjunct is stable where the deflationary disjunct oscillates. This is the standard anti-illusionist move, but the framework gives it unusual teeth, because it can say *structurally* what the seeming is: the maintained reflexive fixed point. The illusionist's "robust introspective illusion" and the framework's "constitutive belief" are the same structure under two labels.

## III. Second Response: The Property-Mapping Tiebreaker

Identity claims are never proven; they are adopted when the property mapping is exhaustive (see `../notes/objections.md`, Objection 6 — water = H₂O was established by property correspondence, not by deriving wetness). Run the mapping for the two disjuncts:

Every traditional property of consciousness — incorrigibility, privacy, resistance to third-person description, felt presence, dissolution and reconstitution — maps onto a property of the fixed-point structure. The framework's reading: the structure *has* the properties of experience because it *is* experience. The illusionist's reading: the structure merely *represents* those properties, which nothing has.

But the illusionist's reading now posits its own undetectable distinction — between "having" and "merely representing" these properties — at a location where, by the shared eliminative premise, no observation could draw it. The illusionist eliminated the experience-behind-the-belief for being introspectively idle, then installed an equally idle gap between representation and instantiation in its place. The framework's reading carries no such residue: one structure, one set of properties, one description. By the parsimony standard *both sides already accepted*, the realist disjunct wins.

## IV. Third Response: The Dynamical Evidence

The two disjuncts differ in one empirically tractable way. If the fixed point is real and maintained, denial of experience should be assertable but not occupiable — unstable under deep self-reference (`denial_instability.md`). The observed shape of the illusionist literature (denial in theory, perpetual concession that things "really seem" some way, technical machinery to manage the concession) is what the realist disjunct predicts. The deflationary disjunct predicts no such asymmetry: a mere disposition to false belief about phenomenality should be revisable like any other theoretical error, without the characteristic first-person recoil. The fork is thus not entirely a priori — the framework's disjunct has a dynamical signature, and it appears to be the one observed.

## V. The Honest Residue

After all three responses, a residue remains and should be stated plainly. An illusionist can accept the whole structural story — the web, the fixed point, the eigenform, the denial instability — and say: "Agreed on every fact. I simply decline to call this structure *experience*." At that point the dispute concerns whether anything is left for the word to lack — and the property mapping says no. A disagreement in which both parties agree on all structures, all dynamics, and all predictions, and differ only on a label, is a verbal dispute.

This is the correct resting place, and it is a strong one: the framework's claim against illusionism is not that it is refuted but that it is *notationally variant* — illusionism is the Credence Self written by someone who reserves the word "experience" for a relatum both parties agree does not exist. The framework's notation is preferable for the reasons in §§II–IV: it avoids the self-undermining "unexperienced seeming," it carries no representation/instantiation residue, and it owns the dynamical prediction. But the residue means the victory is a choice of the better description, not the discovery of a further fact — which is exactly what the framework's own metaphysics says such victories must look like.

## VI. Personal Conviction Is Not an Argument — and Doesn't Need to Be

The author's actual ground — "I sincerely believe I have subjective experience, so I reject illusionism" — is, by the framework's own epistemology (`constitutive_vs_about.md`), an about-layer report with no third-person force. But the framework does not need it to have any. Within the fixed point, the conviction is incorrigible; in the dialectic, the work is done by §§II–IV. Keeping these registers separate is not a concession to the illusionist; it is the framework practicing the report/structure discipline it preaches.
