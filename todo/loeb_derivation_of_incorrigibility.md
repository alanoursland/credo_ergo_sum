# Task: Turn the Löb analogy into a theorem

**Status:** OPEN
**Origin:** Credo §4.1 (Löbian structure); flagged as the freshest, highest-ore idea.
**Depends on:** `introspective_ground.md`, `defining_belief.md`

## The question
The Löb application is currently an *analogy*: "if it were provable it
would be true" → self-validating belief. Löb's theorem requires a
provability predicate satisfying the Hilbert–Bernays–Löb derivability
conditions. Does a cognitive system's self-model actually satisfy those
conditions, or only resemble them?

## Why it matters
This is the one place the identity claim could be *earned* rather than
posited — in exactly one case. If "I believe I am experiencing" can be
shown self-ratifying because the self-model meets the derivability
conditions, then incorrigibility is a theorem, not a stipulation, and
the asserted-not-earned objection is fully retired for that case.

## What would resolve it
- Map □ onto a concrete predicate over the system's self-model.
- Check each derivability condition (□A→□□A, □(A→B)→(□A→□B), necessitation)
  against that predicate. Either they hold (→ theorem) or one fails (→
  the analogy is decorative and should be downgraded in the notes).

## First moves
1. Write the self-model's "provability" predicate explicitly in terms of U.
2. Test the diagonal/necessitation step first — it is the most likely to fail.
3. If it fails, record *which* condition fails; that failure is itself
   informative about what kind of self-reference experience is.
