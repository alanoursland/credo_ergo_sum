# Task: Is the fixed point substrate-real or frame-relative?

**Status:** OPEN — the load-bearing defence against the qFEP attack.
**Origin:** Sandved-Smith et al. §3, §6.1 (unevidenceable boundary; emptiness
as contextuality).
**Depends on:** `mpe_confirmation_or_threat.md`, `nontriviality_derived.md`

## The challenge
The Credo's fixed point is a fixed point *of a bounded system's*
self-reference — it needs the system/world cut to define "the belief takes
itself as input." Fields & Glazebrook prove no finite agent can evidence
its own boundary; the boundary is QRF-relative and ontologically empty.
Then the Credo's fixed point is a fixed point *relative to a chosen QRF*,
and incorrigibility weakens to incorrigible-*given-a-sectorisation*
(P(ō|M_Q) = P(ō|Q,M)) — the exact conditional form they use to defang
separation.

## The candidate answer (make rigorous)
The fixed point is not a claim about *where* the boundary is (agreed:
frame-relative *which* sectorisation). It is a claim about the *reflexive
closure* of whatever system is modelling — and closure is a property that
survives reparametrisation. A whirlpool's boundary is frame-relative; the
whirlpool is still a real dynamical structure. Closure ≠ boundary-placement.

## What resolves it
- Define reflexive closure in a way that is *invariant* under QRF choice.
- Show it survives their contextuality argument (§6.1): contextuality
  attacks value-assignment to a boundary, not the existence of a closed
  interaction pattern.
- If closure cannot be made frame-invariant, the Credo must concede the
  fixed point is frame-relative and rebuild incorrigibility as conditional.
